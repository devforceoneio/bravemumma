Bravemumma

The birth of a brave new era

About

Beautiful, raw, terrifying and sometimes traumatic, giving birth and becoming a mother is an act that changes a woman forever. Bravemumma is a platform to question the way we view and talk about childbirth. It aims to empower women with the information they need to make informed decisions before childbirth and beyond.

  • Before Bravemumma

    As a teacher, triathlete and small business owner, I was always up for a challenge. But my journey to becoming a mumma was the hardest challenge I have ever faced. First came a cancer diagnosis when I was at the peak of my career that threatened to put an end to my dreams of starting a family. Next came years of navigating the unknown around infertility. And finally came the traumatic birth injury that led me to Bravemumma.

  • A brave new era is born

    There’s nothing I’m more grateful for in this world than my healthy children. But because of my injury, every single day of my life needs to be carefully planned and managed to minimise my pain and help me be the best mumma I can be. For years I coped with the invisible side effects and pain that came as a result of my traumatic birth injury - in silence. Searching for resolve in all the wrong places, I explored legal proceedings, TV exposés and surgery.


    Finally, when I thought I was all out of options, Bali found me. At a writer’s retreat, I was given the most profound and life-changing experience. I was given permission to step into what bravery truly means and become the author of my own story. I came home and created Bravemumma and began the long, healing process of telling my story in The Day My Vagina Broke

  • Changing childbirth for the better - with one small win at a time

    I was told that the trauma I experienced at my birth was avoidable. Had I been listened to throughout my pregnancy and assessed as an individual, not simply as one-size-fits-most case, my injury could have been prevented. While I would like to make sure no one ever has to experience what I did, I know these issues go beyond just me - one mumma. Today I’m working hard to be brave enough to speak out about my experiences in the hope that other mummas can have an unbiased view of childbirth and not feel blindsided like I did. This is a huge task and something I can’t do alone. It will take other parents finding their own bravery and speaking out about their experiences to truly start to see a change.


    So, I’m starting conversations and asking hard questions about why we have a birthing culture of judgement that gives power to the hypocrisy embedded within these two paradigms of vaginal and Caesarean births. I want to help create an unbiased environment where midwives and obstetricians support and educate women and their partners about childbirth in a realistic and loving way, with no hidden, political or personal agendas. A culture where the outcomes for babies and their mummas are at the forefront of care. So that women feel empowered to make informed decisions before childbirth, and beyond. I’m also working to help others who’ve had experiences like mine through an online mummahood full of women lifting each other up and supporting each other to heal.

The Day My Vagina Broke

The Day My Vagina Broke is a private exploration into bravery and healing when the impacts of trauma are lifelong and permanent. It also makes the case for moving away from a one-size-fits-many approach to how we inform mothers during the antenatal period and shift towards a model of individualised assessment and care. I wrote it for myself, for my daughter and for all women, everywhere.

The Author

First and foremost, Stephanie is an awesome and brave mumma (and wife). The long journey to becoming a mum is her greatest achievement. Her ‘never-die’ attitude and resilience to adversity was never going to let cancer or birth trauma stop her from trying to be the ‘best’ mum she can be. Stephanie lives with her loving husband, two little people and two fat cats on the South Coast of New South Wales.

When she manages to find a spare 32 minutes, she enjoys watching the ABC's The Let Down, while folding the Mt.Washmore of baby clothes with a cup of tea.
